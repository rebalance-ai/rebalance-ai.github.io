Efficient Reasoning with Balanced Thinking

Abstract

Balanced thinking refers to eliminating redundant reasoning while retaining sufficient exploration to guarantee correctness.

Large Reasoning Models (LRMs) have shown remarkable reasoning capabilities, yet they often suffer from overthinking, expending redundant computational steps on simple problems, or underthinking, failing to explore sufficient reasoning paths despite inherent capabilities. These issues lead to inefficiencies and potential inaccuracies, limiting practical deployment in resource-constrained settings. Existing methods to mitigate overthinking, such as suppressing reflective keywords or adjusting reasoning length, may inadvertently induce underthinking, compromising accuracy. Therefore, we propose ReBalance, a training-free framework that achieves efficient reasoning with balanced thinking. ReBalance leverages confidence as a continuous indicator of reasoning dynamics, identifying overthinking through high confidence variance and underthinking via consistent overconfidence. By aggregating hidden states from a small-scale dataset into reasoning mode prototypes, we compute a steering vector to guide LRMs' reasoning trajectories. A dynamic control function modulates this vector's strength and direction based on real-time confidence, pruning redundancy during overthinking, and promoting exploration during underthinking. Extensive experiments conducted on four models ranging from 0.5B to 32B, and across nine benchmarks in math reasoning, general question answering, and coding tasks demonstrate that ReBalance effectively reduces output redundancy while improving accuracy, offering a general, training-free, and plug-and-play strategy for efficient and robust LRM deployment.

Why ReBalance

ReBalance simultaneously addresses the two failure modes of LRMs: redundant overthinking and premature underthinking.

Balanced Thinking Unlocking Smarter Reasoning

Given the question "For what real values of $x$ is $-4 < x^{4} + 4x^{2} < 21$?", the model first obtains intervals $(-\sqrt{3}, 0)$ and $(0, \sqrt{3})$, and then verifies whether $x = 0$ is included. However, the model redundantly checks irrelevant values after correctly validating $x = 0$, causing overthinking. Current mitigation methods overly suppress necessary reflection, leading to underthinking. ReBalance dynamically controls the reasoning state, effectively balancing these two extremes.

Superior Performance

ReBalance outperforms previous state-of-the-art methods across multiple mathematical reasoning datasets and model scales (0.5B–32B), reducing reasoning length while simultaneously improving accuracy.

Overthinking: redundant reasoning steps and delayed answer convergence.
Underthinking: premature commitment to incorrect reasoning paths.
Balanced Thinking (ReBalance): concise and sufficient reasoning.

Motivation: Why Existing Mitigation Is Not Enough

Shorter reasoning is not always better: reducing overthinking can inadvertently induce underthinking.

Length Distributions

Balanced reduction matters

A good method should reduce redundant reasoning for easy samples without collapsing reasoning depth for hard cases. ReBalance achieves this balance better than previous methods.

Confidence Dynamics

Confidence reveals reasoning state

High confidence variance indicates frequent path switching (overthinking), while consistent overconfidence can lead to premature commitment to incorrect reasoning paths (underthinking).

Design Goal

Continuous and reliable control

Instead of retaining or discarding the entire reasoning path, ReBalance continuously modulates reasoning trajectories using confidence-aware dynamic steering.

Method Pipeline

Offline one-pass data collection + online dynamic steering, connected by confidence signals.

Offline Stage

One-pass data collection

on a small-scale seen dataset, at each step, the steering vector is extracted at the first token of the specified layer based on confidence, and a dynamic function is fitted according to model behaviors.

Online Stage

Inference with Dynamic Steering

During deployment, the dynamic function outputs steering weights based on the model's real-time confidence online, thus balancing between overthinking and underthinking.

Practical Benefit

Training-free and plug-and-play

No extra model training is required. The mechanism can be attached to different reasoning models and tasks with low deployment overhead.

1

Compute confidence signals

For each reasoning step, compute stepwise confidence and local-window confidence variance.

2

Model reasoning modes

Use quantile thresholds to classify steps into overthinking set O and underthinking set U.

3

Extract steering vector

Aggregate deep-layer hidden states into prototypes and define steering from underthinking to overthinking.

4

Apply dynamic control

At each step, adapt steering direction and strength based on current confidence and variance.

Dynamic steering weight:  \( g(c_s,v_s)=\underbrace{\mathrm{sign}\!\big(c_s-\tau_c^{\mathrm{H}}\big)}_{\text{Steering direction}\,\delta_s} \cdot \underbrace{B(c_s,v_s)\,\tanh\!\Big(\big|c_s-\tau_c^{\mathrm{H}}\big|\Big)}_{\text{Steering strength}\,\lambda_s} \)
